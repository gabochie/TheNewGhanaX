Trap #2

Authority Worship

Once someone is in power, they're untouchable

In Ghanaian culture, elders, pastors, politicians, and those in positions of authority are often treated as untouchable. Questioning them isn't just discouraged—it's seen as rebellion, disrespect, or even sin.

What It Is

In Ghanaian culture, elders, pastors, politicians, and those in positions of authority are often treated as **untouchable**. Questioning them isn't just discouraged—it's seen as rebellion, disrespect, or even sin.

The Effect

Leaders operate without scrutiny because no one will speak up. Bad decisions go unchallenged, corruption continues unchecked, and entire communities suffer because accountability is treated as disrespect.

Real-Life Symptoms

  • Blind loyalty during elections—"my party right or wrong"
  • Silence in churches when pastors clearly err or exploit
  • Respectful inability to challenge bad decisions at work
  • Elder worship that dismisses legitimate concerns from youth
  • Teachers/parents who cannot be questioned, even when wrong
  • Corruption that continues because "who are you to ask?"

Why It Persists

Deeply rooted in colonial structures that taught obedience to authority, traditional hierarchies that conflate age with wisdom, religious frameworks that equate authority with divine will, and fear of social ostracism for being labeled "troublemaker."

How to Break Free

  1. Separate person from position — Respect the role, question the individual in it.
  2. Ask "why?" three times — Don't accept first explanations. Dig deeper.
  3. Demand evidence — "Show me the data," "Where's the proof?"
  4. Support questioners — When others speak up, back them publicly.
  5. Model invitation — When you're in authority, explicitly invite questions.

The Shift

From: "Who am I to question?"
To: "Who are they to avoid accountability?"
