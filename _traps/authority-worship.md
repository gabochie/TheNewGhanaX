## Description {#description}

In Ghanaian culture, elders, pastors, politicians, and those in positions of authority are often treated as **untouchable**. Questioning them isn't just discouraged—it's seen as rebellion, disrespect, or even sin.

## The Effect {#the-effect}

No one asks the hard questions. Accountability is reframed as rebellion. Leaders operate without scrutiny, and systems rot from the inside because no one will speak up.

## Real-Life Symptoms {#real-life-symptoms}

- **Blind loyalty** during elections—"my party right or wrong"
- **Silence in churches** when pastors clearly err or exploit
- **Respectful inability** to challenge bad decisions at work
- **Elder worship** that dismisses legitimate concerns from youth
- **Teachers/parents** who cannot be questioned, even when wrong
- **Corruption** that continues because "who are you to ask?"

## Why It Persists {#why-it-persists}

This pattern has roots in:
- **Colonial structures** where questioning authority was punished
- **Traditional respect hierarchies** that conflate age with wisdom
- **Religious frameworks** that equate authority with divine will
- **Fear of ostracism**—those who question become "troublemakers"

## How to Break Free {#how-to-break-free}

1. **Separate the person from the position** — Respect positions of service. Question the people holding them.

2. **Ask "why?" three times** — Don't accept surface answers. Dig deeper.

3. **Demand evidence** — "Because I said so" is never a valid reason from any leader.

4. **Find your voice in low-stakes settings** — Practice questioning in safe spaces before big confrontations.

5. **Support others who speak up** — Create a culture where questioning is normalized.

6. **Be the leader you wish you had** — When you're in authority, invite questions rather than shutting them down.

## The Shift {#the-shift}

From: *"Who am I to question?"*

To: *"Who are they to avoid accountability?"*
